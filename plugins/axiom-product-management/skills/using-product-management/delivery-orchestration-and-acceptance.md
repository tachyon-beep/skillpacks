# Delivery Orchestration and Acceptance

**The owner gets a bet built without building it, and never confuses "engineering says it shipped" with "I verified it landed." DISPATCH hands the committed bet outward to the packs that own delivery; ACCEPT is a gate, not a rubber stamp — work is only ACCEPTED when the owner has seen the thing run against its falsifiable criteria and read the expected value signal. Everything between dispatch and acceptance is someone else's job; the gate at the end is yours alone, and skipping it is how a feature factory feels productive while the business case never realizes.**

This sheet is the DISPATCH and ACCEPT steps of the loop in depth — `product-ownership-operating-model.md` states what those steps *are* and where they sit; this sheet runs them. The two failures it exists to prevent are at opposite ends of the same arc: **dispatch-and-forget** (you hand work out and lose the thread, so nothing ever gets accepted, just silently presumed done) and **the acceptance gap** (you mark work accepted because it shipped, not because it met the criteria you set). Orchestrate, do not implement: this pack writes neither the code nor the plan. It decomposes the bet into dispatchable units, routes each to the pack that owns it, tracks the linkage, and runs the acceptance gate.

## Decompose the bet into dispatchable work

A committed bet from `roadmap.md` is an *outcome* ("reduce checkout abandonment"), not a unit of work. Before anything can be dispatched it has to be broken into pieces each of which has a clear owner-pack, a tracker home, and — for the user-facing pieces — a falsifiable acceptance criterion traceable back to the bet's success criteria in the PDR/PRD. Decomposition here is *just enough to dispatch cleanly*, not a work-breakdown structure: slicing the work into a sequenced backlog is `/axiom-program-management`'s job, and writing the codebase-level task list is `/axiom-planning`'s. The owner's decomposition answers one question per piece: **which pack owns this, and what does "landed" mean for it?**

A clean dispatchable unit has three properties, and a unit missing any of them is not yet ready to dispatch:

- **Exactly one owner-pack.** A unit that needs both a plan and an architecture decision is two units, not one — split it so each lands at a single seam. A unit owned by "program-management *and* planning" has no clear destination and will fall between them.
- **Exactly one meaning-of-landed.** What does "this unit worked" mean, in one testable statement? A unit you cannot state a single landed-condition for is too big or too vague to dispatch; decompose it until each piece has one.
- **A criterion that traces to a PDR clause** (for the value-bearing units). The bet's falsifiable success criteria, set in the PDR/PRD at DECIDE, *flow down* to per-unit criteria: the bet's "activation-within-24h ≥ TARGET" becomes unit C1's acceptance criterion verbatim. This downward flow is where the acceptance gate's testability is actually born — a unit whose criterion does not trace to a recorded bet-criterion is a unit the gate cannot test, because there is nothing falsifiable to test it against (`prd-and-acceptance-criteria.md`). Design/sequencing units (architecture, ordering) carry no value-criterion — they are *enabling*, accepted on delivery, not on a metric.

**The seams between units are surfaced, not managed.** If unit B cannot start until unit A's data model lands, that dependency is a fact you *name* when you dispatch — so `/axiom-program-management` can sequence around it — not a thing you schedule or coordinate yourself. Managing the dependency (the dated, owned commitment between the two) is the sibling's `dependencies-and-coordination.md`; the owner's job is to make the dependency visible at dispatch, then let the pack that owns coordination own it.

The product of decomposition is a short dispatch manifest — generic shape:

| Unit | Owner pack | What it is | Acceptance criterion (traces to PDR) | Tracker |
|------|-----------|------------|--------------------------------------|---------|
| Spec the metered checkout flow | `/write-prd` → `/axiom-planning` | PRD + implementation plan for the top slice | criterion C1: activation-within-24h ≥ TARGET | filigree #M-12 |
| Sequence the slices | `/axiom-program-management` | flow/forecast/WSJF across the slices | n/a (delivery, not value) | (program owns) |
| Shape the data model | `/axiom-solution-architect` | solution/architecture + ADR for the metering store | n/a (design, not value) | GH #240 |
| Build slice 1 | language-engineering pack via the plan | the code | criterion C1 again — verified at ACCEPT | filigree #M-13 |

Two things this manifest is *not*. It is not a duplicate of the backlog — the tracker owns the task detail (`product-state-and-continuity.md`); the manifest holds only the linkage. And it is not a plan — no files, no code, no sequence; those belong to the packs in the "Owner pack" column. The owner's authorship ends at "this unit, that pack, this meaning of done."

## DISPATCH: the routing, by what the unit needs

DISPATCH is the load-bearing seam, and the discipline is mechanical: **match each unit to the pack that owns its mechanics, and route — never restate.** Restating a sibling's mechanics (writing the WSJF table, the flow plan, the implementation steps, the architecture) is the single most common defect in this pack, because it feels like thorough ownership and is actually duplication that drifts out of sync with the real artifact the sibling owns.

| The unit needs… | Route to | What that pack owns | What you hand it |
|-----------------|----------|---------------------|------------------|
| Sequencing, forecast, flow, WSJF, coordination, RAID | **`/axiom-program-management`** | *delivered-predictably* — the order, the date-range, the flow metrics, dependency contracts | the committed bet + its slices; you do not compute WSJF or define cycle time |
| A PRD with falsifiable acceptance criteria | **`/write-prd`** (this pack's command) | the spec the bet hands down | the problem + the bet's success criteria |
| An executable, codebase-validated implementation plan | **`/axiom-planning`** | the ordered task list with exact files and code for the top item | the PRD's top item |
| Solution shape, architecture, ADRs (the *how*) | **`/axiom-solution-architect`** | the design of a chosen thing | the *what/why* and the constraints |
| The build itself | the **language-engineering packs** (via the plan) | the code | the validated plan; you do not write the code |

The order matters: typically `/write-prd` produces the spec, `/axiom-planning` turns its top item into a plan, `/axiom-solution-architect` shapes the *how* when the bet needs design before a plan is sensible, and `/axiom-program-management` sequences the slices and gives the defensible "when." These run in parallel where they are independent — sequencing the slices does not wait on the architecture ADR landing — and the owner's job is to fire each route, not to do the work behind it.

What DISPATCH *records* is the linkage only: in `current-state.md`, each in-flight unit gets a line naming its tracker ID and its owning sibling (`<unit> — dispatched to /axiom-planning — tracker: filigree #M-13`); in the bet's PDR, the dispatch decision and its acceptance criteria. The tracker-adapter contract (`product-state-and-continuity.md`) is how those IDs are created and read — `create(title, type)` to open a unit, `update(id, fields)` as it moves, the stable ID format cited verbatim in the workspace. The rule never bends: the workspace points at the tracker; it does not hold a copy of what the tracker owns.

## Tracking dispatched work without owning the flow

Once work is dispatched, the owner needs to know its status *without* re-running program-management's flow discipline. This is a real tension: dispatch-and-forget is a failure, but so is the owner standing up a parallel cycle-time dashboard and re-deriving throughput — that is `/axiom-program-management`'s `delivery-cadence-and-flow.md`, and reimplementing it here is the same defect as restating WSJF.

The line is this: **the owner tracks *units against acceptance*, program-management tracks *flow*.** The owner's tracking question is never "what is the cycle time" — it is "which dispatched units are now done-by-engineering and therefore *ready for my acceptance gate*, and which are still in flight?" That status comes for free from the tracker adapter at RESUME/ORIENT: `list(filter)` the dispatched IDs, and a unit that the tracker shows closed-by-engineering is a unit that has arrived at the gate. The owner does not need the burndown; the owner needs the arrival signal. When the owner needs a defensible *forecast* of when remaining units will arrive, that is a question for `/axiom-program-management`, asked — not computed here.

A useful discipline: in `current-state.md`, an in-flight unit carries exactly one of three states from the owner's point of view — **dispatched** (handed out, in delivery), **at-gate** (engineering reports done, awaiting the owner's acceptance), **accepted** (the owner ran the gate and it passed) or **rejected** (the gate failed). "Done" is not one of the owner's states; it is a thing engineering says, and it maps only to *at-gate*. That vocabulary is the entire point of the next section.

## The acceptance gate: "done" is not "ACCEPTED"

This is the crux of the sheet and the discipline the owner cannot delegate. **"Done" is an engineering claim: the code shipped, the tests pass, the ticket is closed. "ACCEPTED" is an ownership verdict: the owner verified the thing shipped *and* met its falsifiable acceptance criteria *and* shows the expected value signal.** The two are different in kind, not degree. Done is about output; ACCEPTED is about outcome. A unit can be unambiguously done and entirely unacceptable — it shipped, it works, and it moved no metric the bet was about, or it moved a guardrail the wrong way.

The owner NEVER rubber-stamps "probably done." The gate has three checks, and a unit is ACCEPTED only if it clears all three:

1. **It shipped.** The thing is actually in production / in the user's hands — not merged, not staged, not "ready to deploy." Confirm against reality (the tracker, the running system), not against an engineering assurance. *Merged is not shipped.*
2. **It meets the falsifiable acceptance criteria.** Take the criteria verbatim from the PDR/PRD (`prd-and-acceptance-criteria.md` defines how they were written to be testable) and test the shipped thing against each. Criterion C1 said "activation-within-24h ≥ TARGET against BASELINE"; you read `metrics.md`, you check the actual reading against the actual target. A criterion you cannot test is a gate you cannot pass — kick it back as an acceptance gap, do not wave it through.
3. **It shows the expected value signal — and no guardrail regressed.** The criteria are *leading*; the value signal is whether the bet's hypothesis is being borne out in the north-star/input metrics, and whether any guardrail moved the wrong way (`metrics.md`, `product-metrics-and-experimentation.md`). A unit that hit its acceptance criterion but tanked a guardrail has not landed value — it traded one number for a worse one.

The value signal *lags*: you ship today, the north-star moves over the following weeks. This creates a real trap — wait for the lagging signal and you block acceptance of a clean ship indefinitely; skip it and you are back in the acceptance gap, banking output as outcome. Neither is right. The resolution: when a unit has shipped and its *leading* criteria are met but the value signal is not yet readable, ACCEPT the *ship* and open the value confirmation as its own tracked item with a read-by date (the PDR's reversal trigger usually already names the window — "if activation stays below TARGET for two readings, revisit"). That confirmation item returns to the gate when the date arrives, and *that* later reading is what produces the ACCEPTED / ACCEPTED-PARTIAL / FALSIFIED verdict on the *bet* — the bridge to the kill/keep call in `product-metrics-and-experimentation.md`. Accepting the ship is not rubber-stamping the value; it is recording that the output landed and the outcome verdict is *scheduled*, not skipped.

The verdict is recorded, not assumed. The possible verdicts:

- **ACCEPTED** — all three checks pass. Record it; close the unit; update `metrics.md` with the reading that proved it.
- **ACCEPTED-PARTIAL** — shipped and clears its criteria, but the value signal is weaker than the hypothesis predicted (the bet half-worked). This is *information*, not a pass: record a PDR, and open a follow-up bet to investigate the gap rather than banking the unit as a clean win.
- **FALSIFIED** — shipped clean, criteria met, and the value signal says the *bet was wrong*. This is a **successful product outcome**: you learned the bet was wrong cheaply. Record it in a PDR (the reversal trigger fired), and the kill/double-down call goes to `product-metrics-and-experimentation.md`. Do not bury a falsified bet as a failure; an unfalsifiable bet is the worse outcome.
- **REJECTED** — it did not ship, or it failed a criterion, or it regressed a guardrail. Back to delivery with the specific failed check named; do not accept-with-a-note.

A worked gate, generic. Bet ALPHA's PRD set criterion C1 = "activation-within-24h ≥ TARGET (BASELINE was lower)" and named a guardrail "p95 latency ≤ ceiling." Engineering closes the unit: **done.** The owner runs the gate. *Shipped?* Tracker shows it live in production — yes. *Criteria?* `metrics.md` reads activation at TARGET — C1 passes. *Value signal + guardrails?* North-star ticked up as predicted, but p95 latency now reads above ceiling. Verdict: **REJECTED** — the bet traded activation for a latency regression that breaches a guardrail; it is not ACCEPTED no matter how done it is. The owner records the verdict, names the guardrail breach as the failed check, and routes it back. Had latency held, the same evidence would have produced **ACCEPTED**. The point of the worked case: *done was never in question; acceptance was, and only the owner's gate resolves it.*

## Where this sits in the loop

DISPATCH and ACCEPT are not adjacent in time — a unit is dispatched in one session and arrives at the gate one or many sessions later. This is exactly why the tracking discipline matters: the linkage recorded at DISPATCH in `current-state.md` is what a future RESUME reads to know a unit is in flight, and the tracker's closed-by-engineering signal at ORIENT is what flags it as at-gate. ACCEPT then runs against the criteria recorded back at DECIDE — which is why those criteria had to be falsifiable when they were written, not retrofitted now. The gate cannot be stronger than the criteria it tests; an acceptance gap that looks like it opened at ACCEPT usually opened at DECIDE, when a vague criterion was let through (`prd-and-acceptance-criteria.md`).

## Anti-Patterns

1. **Dispatch-and-forget (the open-ended dispatch).** Work is handed to a sibling pack and the thread is dropped; no linkage is recorded, no arrival signal is watched, and the unit is silently presumed done because no one heard otherwise. Seductive because dispatching *feels* like the work is now handled — it has an owner that isn't you. But "handed out" is not "landed," and a unit with no recorded linkage is one the next RESUME cannot even see is in flight, let alone bring to the gate. *Fix: record the tracker ID + owning sibling in `current-state.md` at DISPATCH, and track units to the gate via the tracker adapter (`product-state-and-continuity.md`); the loop's ACCEPT step is not optional.*

2. **The acceptance gap — accepting because it shipped.** A unit is marked accepted because engineering said done and the ticket closed, not because the owner tested it against the falsifiable criteria and the value signal. Seductive under time pressure and because "done" sounds final and trustworthy — challenging a closed ticket feels like distrust. But done is an output claim and ACCEPTED is an outcome verdict; conflating them is precisely the build trap, banking shipped features as value never validated. *Fix: run the three-check gate (shipped / meets criteria / shows value signal with no guardrail regression) and record an explicit verdict; "probably done" is never a verdict (`product-metrics-and-experimentation.md`, `prd-and-acceptance-criteria.md`).*

3. **Owner-as-implementer.** The owner, impatient with the seam, writes the implementation plan, the architecture, or the code itself — or specifies the work in such detail that the dispatch manifest *is* a plan. Seductive because the owner often *could* do it and dispatching feels like overhead between the owner and the result. But it collapses the seam that makes the pack coherent, duplicates what the engineering packs own, and leaves the owner holding work no acceptance gate was designed to check. *Fix: decompose only to the "which pack, what does landed mean" level; hand the plan to `/axiom-planning`, the design to `/axiom-solution-architect`, the build to the engineering packs (`product-ownership-operating-model.md`).*

4. **Reimplementing program-management's flow discipline.** To "track" dispatched work, the owner stands up a cycle-time view, re-derives throughput, or computes a delivery forecast — re-running the discipline `/axiom-program-management` owns under the banner of staying on top of delivery. Seductive because tracking-to-the-gate and tracking-flow look similar and both feel like diligence. But the owner's tracking question is "which units have arrived at the gate," not "what is the cycle time"; flow is the sibling's object, and rebuilding it here is the same defect as restating WSJF. *Fix: track units against acceptance via the tracker's arrival signal; when you need a defensible forecast, *ask* `/axiom-program-management` — do not compute it (`product-ownership-operating-model.md`).*

5. **Decomposition that is really a backlog.** The dispatch manifest grows task detail, sequence, and estimates until it is a second copy of the tracker backlog living in the workspace. Seductive because more detail feels like more control. It guarantees the workspace and the tracker diverge — two sources of truth for one backlog — and drags the owner back into program-management's slicing job. *Fix: the manifest holds linkage only (unit → pack → meaning-of-landed → tracker ID); slicing into a sequenced backlog is `/axiom-program-management`, task detail is `/axiom-planning` (`product-state-and-continuity.md`).*
