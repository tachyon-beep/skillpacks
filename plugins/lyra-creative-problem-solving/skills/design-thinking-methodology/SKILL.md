# Design Thinking Methodology

## Overview

Design thinking is a structured, human-centered approach to innovation and problem-solving. It provides a 5-phase framework that moves from understanding problems to testing solutions systematically.

**Core Principle**: Understand people's needs deeply before solving, generate many options before choosing, test assumptions before committing.

**Origin**: Developed at Stanford d.school and IDEO, design thinking has become the dominant framework for innovation across tech, business, and social sectors.

## When to Use

Use design thinking when:
- Designing new products, features, or services
- Solving complex problems with unclear requirements
- User needs are ambiguous or unknown
- Need systematic innovation process
- Running innovation workshops or sprints
- Problem requires human-centered solutions

**Don't use for**:
- Well-defined technical problems with clear specifications
- Pure optimization tasks (use constraint-based-creativity instead)
- Situations where user research is impossible or inappropriate

## The Five Phases

```
Empathize → Define → Ideate → Prototype → Test
   ↑                                        ↓
   └────────── Iterate ←─────────────────────┘
```

Design thinking is iterative—insights from testing often require returning to earlier phases.

---

## Phase 1: Empathize

**Goal**: Deeply understand the people you're designing for.

### Core Activities

**User Interviews** (1-on-1 conversations):
- Ask open-ended questions: "Tell me about the last time you..."
- Focus on behavior, not opinions: "What did you do?" not "What would you do?"
- Follow interesting threads: "Tell me more about that..."
- Listen for workarounds, frustrations, delights

**Observation** (watching behavior in context):
- Shadow users in their natural environment
- Note what they do vs what they say
- Watch for workarounds (signals of unmet needs)
- Photograph artifacts, spaces, tools they use

**Immersion** (experience it yourself):
- Use the current solution/process yourself
- Feel the friction points directly
- Build empathy through direct experience

### Deliverables

- Interview transcripts or detailed notes
- Photos of user environments, artifacts, workarounds
- Empathy maps (what users Say/Think/Do/Feel)
- Journey maps showing current experience over time

### Common Mistakes

❌ **Asking leading questions**: "Don't you think this feature would be useful?"
✅ **Open exploration**: "How do you currently handle X?"

❌ **Only talking to accessible users**: Interview only power users or early adopters
✅ **Diverse perspectives**: Talk to frustrated users, non-users, edge cases

❌ **Confirmation bias**: Looking for data that supports your idea
✅ **Genuine curiosity**: Seeking to understand, not validate

## Phase 2: Define

**Goal**: Synthesize insights into a clear, actionable problem statement.

### Core Activities

**Synthesis** (making sense of research):
- Affinity diagramming: Cluster related observations
- Identify patterns across users
- Find tensions, contradictions, surprising insights
- Distinguish symptoms from root causes

**Point-of-View (POV) Statement**:

```
[User] needs [need] because [insight]
```

**Example**:
- ❌ Generic: "Users need better tools"
- ✅ Specific: "Busy parents need quick meal planning because they lack time and mental energy after work, not meal ideas"

**"How Might We" (HMW) Questions**:

Transform POV into actionable questions:

```
POV: "Commuters need reliable arrival time estimates because they're anxious about being late to important meetings"

HMW Questions:
- How might we reduce arrival time uncertainty?
- How might we give commuters peace of mind about being on time?
- How might we help commuters communicate delays proactively?
- How might we make waiting less stressful?
```

### Deliverables

- Point-of-View statement
- 5-10 "How Might We" questions
- Prioritized problem focus for ideation

### Common Mistakes

❌ **Solution jumping**: "Users need a mobile app"
✅ **Problem focus**: "Users need access on the go"

❌ **Too broad**: "Users need better experiences"
✅ **Specific insight**: "Users need confidence that data won't be lost during unstable network connections"

❌ **Premature convergence**: Picking one HMW question too early
✅ **Multiple framings**: Explore 5-10 different HMW angles before choosing

## Phase 3: Ideate

**Goal**: Generate many diverse solution ideas before evaluating.

### Core Principles

1. **Defer judgment** - No criticism during generation
2. **Encourage wild ideas** - Unconventional thinking welcomed
3. **Build on others** - "Yes, and..." not "Yes, but..."
4. **Quantity over quality** - Aim for 50-100+ ideas
5. **Stay visual** - Sketch, don't just talk

### Techniques

**Classic Brainstorming**:
- 7-minute sprints per HMW question
- Individual silent generation first (prevents groupthink)
- Share and build collectively
- Aim for 20+ ideas per question

**Worst Possible Idea**:
- "How might we make this problem worse?"
- Generates hilarious, relaxing ideas
- Often reveals inverse solutions that work

**Analogies**:
- "How would [Disney/NASA/Amazon/Tesla] solve this?"
- "What does nature's solution look like?" (biomimicry)
- Cross-industry inspiration

**Provocation** (from lateral thinking):
- "What if we had infinite resources?"
- "What if we had to do this in 1 day?"
- "What if the opposite were true?"

### Deliverables

- 50-100+ solution concepts (sketches, sticky notes)
- Clustered into themes
- 5-10 directions worth prototyping

### Common Mistakes

❌ **Immediate criticism**: "That won't work because..."
✅ **Defer judgment**: Capture all ideas, evaluate later

❌ **Too few ideas**: Stop at 10 ideas, pick "best" one
✅ **Abundant options**: Generate 50-100, then cluster and prioritize

❌ **Dominant voices**: Loud people control session
✅ **Individual then shared**: Silent generation prevents groupthink

## Phase 4: Prototype

**Goal**: Make ideas tangible to learn and test quickly.

### Core Principles

**Match fidelity to questions**:
- Need to test flow? → Paper sketches
- Need to test visual appeal? → Design mockups
- Need to test technical feasibility? → Code spike

**Prototype to learn, not to impress**:
- Low fidelity is often better (invites feedback)
- Speed matters more than polish
- One prototype per key question

### Fidelity Ladder

| Fidelity | Time | Best For | Examples |
|----------|------|----------|----------|
| **Sketch** | 5 min | Test basic concept, flow | Napkin sketches, wireframes |
| **Paper prototype** | 30 min | Test interaction, navigation | Clickable paper screens |
| **Digital mockup** | 2-4 hrs | Test visual design, layout | Figma/Sketch designs |
| **Clickable prototype** | 1 day | Test realistic interaction | InVision, Framer |
| **Code prototype** | 2-5 days | Test technical feasibility | Working demo (fake backend) |
| **MVP** | 2-8 weeks | Test product-market fit | Minimal working product |

### Techniques

**Storyboarding**:
- Comic-strip style: User's journey with your solution
- Highlights context, emotional journey, key interactions
- Great for communicating vision to stakeholders

**Wizard of Oz**:
- Fake the automation/AI/backend manually
- User thinks it's working, you're operating behind scenes
- Tests concept before building infrastructure

**Concierge MVP**:
- Manually deliver the service instead of automating
- Learn what value really looks like
- Example: Food delivery service where founder personally delivers

### Deliverables

- 3-5 prototypes of different fidelities
- Each tests specific assumptions/questions
- Ready for user testing

### Common Mistakes

❌ **Over-polishing**: Spend 3 weeks on high-fidelity prototype before testing
✅ **Quick and dirty**: Test sketches first, increase fidelity only if validated

❌ **One prototype**: Build single solution, no alternatives
✅ **Multiple options**: Test 3-5 different approaches

❌ **Prototype everything**: Try to test every aspect at once
✅ **Focused questions**: Each prototype tests 1-2 specific assumptions

## Phase 5: Test

**Goal**: Get feedback, learn what works, identify improvements.

### Core Principles

**Test with real users**:
- Same people from Empathize phase (if possible)
- Diverse user segments, not just early adopters
- 5-8 users often sufficient to find major issues

**Observe, don't explain**:
- Give minimal instructions
- Watch them struggle (resist urge to help)
- Ask them to think aloud
- Note where they hesitate, make mistakes, get confused

**Test to learn, not to validate**:
- You want to find problems, not confirmation
- "Fail fast" - better to learn now than after building

### Testing Techniques

**Usability Testing**:
```
1. Give scenario: "You want to schedule a meeting for tomorrow"
2. Give prototype: "Use this to accomplish that"
3. Shut up and watch
4. Note where they struggle
5. Ask after: "What were you thinking when you clicked X?"
```

**A/B Testing** (if you have multiple prototypes):
- Show different versions to different users
- Compare which performs better
- Useful for comparing specific variations

**Feedback Interview**:
- Show prototype
- Ask: "What stands out to you?"
- Ask: "What would you change?"
- Ask: "Would you use this? Why/why not?"

### What to Test

**Desirability**: Do people want this?
- "Would you use this?"
- Watch their eyes: Do they light up or glaze over?
- Listen for enthusiasm vs polite interest

**Usability**: Can people use it?
- Do they complete tasks successfully?
- Where do they get stuck?
- How many errors do they make?

**Feasibility**: Can we build this?
- Technical constraints
- Resource constraints
- Time constraints

**Viability**: Should we build this?
- Business model
- Strategic fit
- Opportunity cost

### Deliverables

- Test observations and insights
- "Works well" patterns to keep
- "Needs improvement" areas to iterate
- Decision: Iterate on this direction or pivot to different approach

### Common Mistakes

❌ **Asking "Would you use this?"**: People lie to be nice
✅ **Observe behavior**: Watch if they actually use it successfully

❌ **Explaining the prototype**: "So here's how it works..."
✅ **Let them discover**: Give scenario, shut up, watch

❌ **Defending design**: "Well actually, you're supposed to..."
✅ **Embrace confusion**: If they're confused, the design failed, not them

## Iteration Patterns

Design thinking is non-linear. Here's when to return to earlier phases:

### Test → Empathize
**When**: Users have different needs than you thought
**Action**: More research with different user segments

### Test → Define
**When**: Solving the wrong problem
**Action**: Reframe POV and HMW questions

### Test → Ideate
**When**: Current solutions aren't working, need new directions
**Action**: Brainstorm alternative approaches

### Test → Prototype
**When**: Promising direction, but needs refinement
**Action**: Iterate on fidelity or details

### Define → Empathize
**When**: Can't synthesize clear POV from research
**Action**: More targeted research to fill gaps

## Real-World Process Timelines

### 1-Day Sprint
- Empathize: 2 hours (review existing research or quick interviews)
- Define: 1 hour (synthesis, POV, HMW)
- Ideate: 2 hours (brainstorm)
- Prototype: 2 hours (sketches, paper prototypes)
- Test: 1 hour (quick feedback from 3-5 people)

### 1-Week Sprint
- Empathize: 2 days (10-15 user interviews)
- Define: 1 day (synthesis workshop)
- Ideate: 1 day (extensive brainstorming)
- Prototype: 1 day (multiple low-fi prototypes)
- Test: 1 day (5-8 user tests)

### 4-Week Project
- Empathize: Week 1 (20+ interviews, observations)
- Define: Week 2 (deep synthesis, multiple POVs)
- Ideate: Week 2-3 (multiple brainstorm sessions)
- Prototype: Week 3 (low to medium fidelity)
- Test: Week 4 (structured testing with 15+ users)

**Adjust based on problem complexity and stakes.**

## Quick Reference: Phase Checklist

### ✓ Empathize
- [ ] Interviewed 8-15 users from diverse segments
- [ ] Observed users in natural context
- [ ] Documented with photos, quotes, artifacts
- [ ] Created empathy maps or journey maps

### ✓ Define
- [ ] Synthesized research into patterns and insights
- [ ] Created specific POV statement (User/Need/Insight)
- [ ] Generated 5-10 "How Might We" questions
- [ ] Prioritized 1-3 HMW questions to explore

### ✓ Ideate
- [ ] Generated 50-100+ ideas across HMW questions
- [ ] Used multiple ideation techniques
- [ ] Deferred judgment during generation
- [ ] Clustered ideas into themes
- [ ] Selected 3-5 directions to prototype

### ✓ Prototype
- [ ] Created prototypes at appropriate fidelity
- [ ] Each prototype tests specific assumptions
- [ ] Made 3-5 prototypes of different approaches
- [ ] Prototypes are testable with users

### ✓ Test
- [ ] Tested with 5-8 real users
- [ ] Gave scenarios, observed behavior
- [ ] Noted struggles, confusion, success
- [ ] Identified what works and what needs iteration
- [ ] Decided: iterate, pivot, or proceed

## Common Anti-Patterns

### ❌ Fake Empathy
**Symptom**: Skip research, "put yourself in users' shoes"
**Why Bad**: You are not your user. Your assumptions are wrong.
**Fix**: Actually talk to real users.

### ❌ Solution in Search of Problem
**Symptom**: Start with solution idea, retrofit problem to fit
**Why Bad**: Solves wrong problem, ignores user needs
**Fix**: Start with Empathize and Define before Ideate

### ❌ Analysis Paralysis
**Symptom**: Spend weeks in Empathize/Define, never build anything
**Why Bad**: Learning through testing > learning through thinking
**Fix**: Set time limits, move to Prototype faster

### ❌ Premature Convergence
**Symptom**: Generate 5 ideas, pick one, build it fully
**Why Bad**: Miss better solutions, over-invest before validation
**Fix**: Generate 50+ ideas, prototype 3-5, test before committing

### ❌ Prototype Theater
**Symptom**: Build beautiful high-fidelity mockups, skip testing
**Why Bad**: Wasted time on polish before validating direction
**Fix**: Test ugly sketches first, increase fidelity only if validated

### ❌ Ignoring Test Results
**Symptom**: Users struggle with prototype, but you build it anyway
**Why Bad**: Ship something users can't/won't use
**Fix**: Iterate based on feedback or pivot to different approach

## Real-World Examples

### Airbnb Turnaround (2009)
**Problem**: Bookings plateaued, company near failure
**Empathize**: Founders visited hosts, saw terrible photos hurting bookings
**Define**: HMW make listings more appealing?
**Ideate**: Professional photography, better descriptions, verified hosts
**Prototype**: Founders personally photographed listings in NYC
**Test**: Bookings 2x-3x for photographed listings
**Result**: Professional photography service, company growth trajectory changed

### IDEO Shopping Cart (Classic Example)
**Problem**: Redesign grocery shopping cart
**Empathize**: Observed shoppers, store employees, parents with kids
**Define**: Multiple HMWs (safety, maneuverability, theft prevention, capacity)
**Ideate**: 100s of ideas from diverse team
**Prototype**: Built full-scale prototypes from different materials
**Test**: Used in real store, gathered feedback
**Result**: Modular cart with better steering, child safety, scanner holder

### Wallet Project (d.school Exercise)
**Problem**: Redesign wallet for partner
**Empathize**: Interview partner about wallet use (5 min)
**Define**: POV based on their specific needs
**Ideate**: Sketch 10 ideas (5 min)
**Prototype**: Build prototype from paper (10 min)
**Test**: Give to partner, get feedback (5 min)
**Insight**: Demonstrates full cycle in 30 minutes

## The Bottom Line

Design thinking provides structure to innovation:
1. Understand people deeply (Empathize)
2. Frame the right problem (Define)
3. Explore many solutions (Ideate)
4. Make ideas tangible (Prototype)
5. Learn through testing (Test)

**Most important**: It's iterative. Testing reveals what to do next—often returning to earlier phases with new insights.

**When stuck**: You're probably skipping phases. Most commonly: Jumping to solutions without Empathize/Define, or building too long without Test.
