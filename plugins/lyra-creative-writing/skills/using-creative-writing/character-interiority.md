# Character Interiority

Interiority is what makes the reader feel they are inside a person rather than watching one. It is also the place where prose most often goes slack — the writer, knowing the character so well, dumps motivation, replays the obvious, narrates the character thinking *about* things instead of letting them think. The discipline is to render minds vividly when you render them at all, and to know when to let the body and the dialogue carry the load instead.

## Three registers of interiority

Interiority is not one thing. It is three, and each wants different prose.

**Thought** — the verbal layer. What the character would say to herself, or almost-say. Rendered well, it has the cadence of speech-not-quite-spoken: *Of course she'd be late. Of course.* Rendered badly, it becomes a ticker-tape of motivation the reader already inferred.

**Feeling** — the emotional layer. Rarely served by naming the emotion (*she felt sad*, *he was furious*); usually served by pressure, by what the feeling *does*. Marilynne Robinson in *Gilead* almost never names a feeling outright; the feeling arrives through the texture of the sentence around it. Stephen King, working a different register entirely in *Misery*, does the same — Paul Sheldon's terror is in the rhythm of the prose, not in the word *terror*.

**Sensation** — the body layer. Heat, pressure, taste, the small physical events the character notices. Often the most economical route to the other two: a clenched jaw renders anger without naming it, a dry mouth renders fear, the way a hand will not quite stop shaking renders something the character themself is refusing to name.

Strong interiority typically braids all three. Weak interiority lives in thought alone, talking *about* feeling and sensation rather than rendering them.

## Distance gates interiority

What you can render depends on where you are sitting. (See `pov-and-voice` for the distance continuum in detail.)

**Close third** can render thought directly, in the character's own diction, without tags. *She would never finish this. Of course she wouldn't.* The interiority is fluid; free indirect discourse does the work invisibly.

**Distant third** must report interiority rather than render it. *She was beginning to suspect she would never finish.* The distance flattens the voice and the emotional charge — sometimes that is exactly what the scene wants.

**Very distant third** can only infer interiority from behaviour. *She set the page down and did not pick it up again.* The reader builds the inner life; the prose does not hand it over.

**First person** bends the rules. The narrator is *always* close to herself, but she may be looking back from years away, in which case the interiority is doubled — the younger self's experience filtered through the older self's understanding. Marilynne Robinson's *Housekeeping*, or Kazuo Ishiguro's *The Remains of the Day*, both run this double channel.

The slip to watch for: close third drifting into reported interiority mid-paragraph (*she felt that perhaps she was beginning to suspect*), or distant third suddenly producing a rendered thought that the distance had not earned.

## What a character notices vs. what is true

Interiority is not a window onto truth. It is a window onto a particular consciousness, which is reliably partial and often wrong. The gap between what the character notices and what is actually happening is one of the deepest tools in close POV.

A character in love misreads a friend's silence as approval. A grieving character cannot see the kindness in front of her. A character defending a self-image reframes every disappointment as someone else's fault. Tana French's first-person detectives in *In the Woods* and *The Likeness* are masterclasses in this: the reader gradually realises the narrator is not seeing what is in plain view.

Render character through *what they fail to see* as much as through what they see. The detail the POV character glides past — that the reader catches — is characterisation. The thing she insists on noticing repeatedly is also characterisation, often of something she will not look at directly.

## Interiority that earns its keep

Interiority is working when it:

- **Moves the scene forward.** The character notices something, reframes something, decides something — and the next beat depends on that motion.
- **Complicates the present moment.** The dialogue says one thing; the interiority shows the character meaning another. The action says calm; the interiority shows the cost of calm.
- **Reveals through omission.** The character refuses to look at the obvious thing, and the refusal *is* the revelation.
- **Earns its register.** A spike of interior intensity at a moment that warrants it; quiet observation at a moment that warrants that.

## Interiority that stalls

Interiority stalls when it:

- **Restates motivation the reader already has.** *She wanted him to leave because she was tired and because of what he had said earlier.* The reader knows. Cut.
- **Loops.** The character thinks the same thought in three slightly different formulations across a paragraph. Pick the sharpest; cut the others.
- **Goes on-the-nose.** *She realised that she was angry because her father had never listened to her either.* Self-analysis at therapy-couch resolution rarely reads as a mind; it reads as the writer explaining the character to the reader. Saunders is sharp on this in *A Swim in a Pond in the Rain*: characters who explain themselves do not feel like people.
- **"Thinks about" rather than thinks.** *She thought about how unfair it all was.* The construction is a tell; the interiority has gone abstract. Render the unfairness, or render the thought; do not narrate the thinking-about.

In revision, scan for *felt*, *realised*, *thought about*, *began to wonder*, *found herself*. Most are filter-word symptoms, the same pattern flagged in `pov-and-voice` slipping into the interior register. Half can be cut outright; the other half want to be replaced with the rendered thing.

## The free-indirect bridge

Interiority and free indirect discourse are not separate techniques. Free indirect discourse is the grammatical machinery; interiority is what that machinery carries. When close third works, it is because thought, feeling, and sensation are leaking into the narration in the character's own voice without ever needing tags.

*[Example crafted for illustration.]* Reported interiority, distant: *She thought the meeting had gone badly and she was worried about what her manager would say.* Rendered interiority, close third via free indirect: *Well, that had gone superbly. Nothing like a forty-minute silence from your manager to clarify things.* The grammar still belongs to the narrator (third person, past tense), but the diction, the sarcasm, the rhythm — all hers. The reader is in her head without ever being told they are.

Jane Austen built this engine; Saunders teaches it; every close-third writer alive uses it. It is the single most efficient route to interiority that does not stall.
