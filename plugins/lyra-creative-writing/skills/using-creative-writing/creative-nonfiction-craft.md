# Creative Nonfiction Craft

Creative nonfiction is the form that uses the fiction writer's tools to render true events. The phrase irritates some practitioners (Annie Dillard preferred "narrative nonfiction"; some journalists prefer "literary journalism"), but the territory is real: memoir, the personal essay, the lyric essay, literary journalism, narrative nonfiction. What unites them is a load-bearing claim — *this happened* — and what divides them from fiction is everything that claim entails.

## CNF and fiction — the overlap

Most fiction craft transfers directly. Scene is scene whether you invented it or lived it. POV and distance work the same way; free indirect discourse can render a remembered self the way it renders a fictional character. Voice, sentence rhythm, paragraph cadence, opening pressure, ending register — all of it. Dialogue carries subtext in CNF as in fiction. Character is character.

This is not a metaphor. Didion's essays are scene-built. Patti Smith's *Just Kids* runs on dramatised time and rendered interiority. Hilton Als's *White Girls* uses every available technique of the lyric novel. The CNF writer's toolkit is the fiction writer's toolkit — minus a few liberties, plus a few obligations.

## CNF and fiction — the divergence

The truth-claim. When the prose says *this happened*, the reader expects it actually happened. That contract is the load-bearing distinction. Break it without disclosure and you are not writing CNF; you are writing fiction wearing a memoir jacket, and the reader has been misled. James Frey's *A Million Little Pieces* did not fail as prose; it failed as contract.

The contract has degrees. Memoir admits to memory's limits. Literary journalism admits to compression and reconstruction. Lyric essay admits to associative leap. But each form operates inside *truthfulness as good faith*, not truthfulness as inviolable transcription.

## Truth-claim vs verisimilitude

Fiction is verisimilar — feels true. CNF claims to *be* true, with caveats. The legitimate space between the two is where craft lives. Memory is partial; a remembered conversation is reconstructed in substance, not transcribed; a scene is shaped for meaning that was not visible at the time. None of this breaks the contract, provided the writer is honest about the practice.

Vivian Gornick, in *The Situation and the Story*, draws the line cleanly: the *situation* is what happened (the facts); the *story* is what the writer makes of them (the meaning). The story is shaped, selected, ordered — and that shaping is craft, not falsification, so long as the situation is not invented.

## Persona vs author

The speaker on the page is not quite the writer. CNF earns that distance. The "I" in *Slouching Towards Bethlehem* is Joan Didion's persona — a particular angle of attention, a constructed voice — not Joan Didion eating breakfast. Gornick is explicit: "every work of literature has both a situation and a story… the writer must figure out who is telling the story." The narrating self is a made thing, even when it is also true.

This is craft, not deception. The persona of the essay or memoir is selective, voiced, positioned. It says *more* than the writer would say at a dinner party and *less* than the writer knows; it has angles the writer has chosen. The reader is not being told the persona is the whole writer. They are being told it is a true voice, honestly constructed.

## Scene-driven CNF

Each form handles dramatised scene differently.

**Memoir.** Scene-built around a remembered self. Patti Smith's *Just Kids* dramatises Chelsea Hotel rooms and after-hours diners; Jeannette Walls's *The Glass Castle* renders childhood in present-tense scene. The discipline is rendering the remembered as if witnessed, while admitting (in the form's overall register) that memory is doing the witnessing.

**Literary journalism.** Scene-built around reported events. John Jeremiah Sullivan's *Pulphead* dramatises Christian rock festivals and reality-TV sets from notes, recordings, and immersion. The scenes are reconstructed from material, not memory; the reportorial work is what earns the dramatisation.

**Lyric and personal essay.** Scene fragments inside an associative argument. Maggie Nelson's *Bluets* and *The Argonauts*, Eula Biss's *Notes from No Man's Land* — the scene is one move among many, threaded with theory, citation, reflection. The shape is essayistic; the moments are scene-rendered when scene is what the meaning needs.

## Reconstructed dialogue

The working conventions are practical and well-established.

**The disclosure norm.** An author's note explaining the practice — that conversations have been reconstructed from memory, that some are composites, that the substance is preserved even where the exact words cannot be. Nearly every reputable memoir does this. The note is the contract's small print.

**What is permitted.** The substance of what was said, in the speaker's idiom — the way they actually talked, their characteristic words, the gist of the exchange. A conversation reconstructed years later cannot be transcript-accurate; CNF readers know this and accept it.

**What is not.** Inventing speech wholesale; attributing to a real person something they did not say or would not say. The grey zone is wide — composites, compression, the smoothing of *um* and *uh* — but the floor is firm: do not put words in a real mouth.

## The ethics of writing about real people

Relatives, exes, colleagues, the dead. Three working questions.

**Permission.** Often impractical (you cannot get consent from everyone in your childhood), sometimes essential (a living spouse, a current colleague). The form does not require permission; the relationship may.

**Consequence.** Who reads this and how does their life change? Mary Karr's *The Liars' Club* and Walls's *The Glass Castle* both faced family fallout. The writer does not owe relatives a flattering portrait; the writer does owe themselves and the reader a depiction that is *fair* — that depicts rather than judges, that lets the subject be a person rather than a position.

**Depiction vs judgement.** Writing *about* someone is the contract. Writing *at* them — settling scores, indicting in prose — is something else. James Baldwin's essays on family and race depict with heat that never collapses into score-settling; the heat is moral attention, not invective.

The autofiction question (Lerner, Heti, Cusk) sits at the edge of this. *10:04*, *How Should a Person Be?*, the *Outline* trilogy — books where the line between memoir and fiction is the form. The autofiction writer makes the contract negotiable on purpose. That is a legitimate move; it is not the same move as memoir, and CNF craft holds the line elsewhere.

## "Emotional truth" — earned vs excuse

Legitimate use: memory is shaped, scenes are compressed for meaning, two evenings become one because the meaning runs through both, dialogue is approximate because nobody recorded it. This is the form working as designed.

The misuse: rewriting what happened to make a better story while still claiming it as nonfiction. Inventing a confrontation that did not occur, sharpening an exchange to make a cleaner arc, attributing to oneself a wisdom one did not have at the time. *Emotional truth* in this register is the alibi for fiction with the wrong label. The honest move when the better story is the invented one is to write the novel.
