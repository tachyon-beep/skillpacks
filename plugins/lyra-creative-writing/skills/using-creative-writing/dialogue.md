# Dialogue

Dialogue is where character is most exposed and most easily faked. Two characters in a room talking will reveal how much the writer knows about them, and how much the writer is hoping the reader will not notice. Dialogue rewards revision more reliably than almost any other element of prose; most early-draft dialogue is doing roughly half the work it could be doing, and the half it is missing is the half that matters.

## Voice differentiation

Two characters should not sound alike, but the tools are subtler than accent or catchphrase. *He always says "right then"* is a tag, not a voice. Real differentiation lives in:

- **Diction** — Latinate vs Anglo-Saxon, formal vs colloquial, jargon vs plain. A surgeon and a fisherman draw on different word-stocks even discussing the weather.
- **Syntax** — sentence length, clause structure, willingness to leave things grammatically unfinished. Some characters speak in complete sentences; some never do.
- **Rhythm** — where the stresses fall, how often they pause, whether they hedge before the verb or after it.
- **What they notice** — dialogue is shaped by what a character's attention lands on. The accountant notices cost; the carpenter notices grain.
- **What they refuse to say** — and how. Characters who deflect through irony sound nothing like characters who deflect through changing the subject.

*[Example crafted for illustration]* Two characters in a stalled lift. **Mira:** "It's the relay. Twenty minutes, tops, before someone notices the alarm panel." **Joe:** "Yeah. Or, you know. Could be a while." Same situation, same information. Mira diagnoses then forecasts; Joe shrugs through hedges. No accent, no catchphrase — but you can tell whose line is whose without an attribution.

George V. Higgins built whole novels on this — *The Friends of Eddie Coyle* differentiates a dozen voices through cadence and refusal, never costume.

## Subtext — said vs. meant

The gap between what a character says and what they mean is dialogue's primary engine. When the said and the meant align, dialogue is information transfer; when they diverge, dialogue is drama.

*[Example crafted for illustration]* **"Do you want me to stay?"** is a different line in every relationship. From the visitor hoping to be released, it means *please tell me I can go.* From the visitor afraid of the answer, it means *please ask me to.* From the visitor who has already decided, it means *I am about to leave whether you say yes or no.* The line is identical; the subtext is the scene.

Tana French's interrogation scenes work this way — questions and answers are the surface; the territory underneath is where the chapter happens. Same in Sally Rooney, where polite conversation conducts entire relationships beneath the politeness.

## Attribution rhythm

*Said* is invisible. The reader's eye glides past it. *Asked* is nearly invisible. Almost every other attribution verb — *muttered, hissed, exclaimed, gasped, retorted* — calls attention to itself and away from the line. Use the stronger verb only when its specific information is unavailable from the line.

Beats often work better than tags: an action attributing the speaker through who is doing it. *He set the cup down. "Then I suppose we're finished."* No *he said* required; the beat does the attribution and adds physical texture.

In multi-speaker exchanges: tag the first one or two lines, then drop attribution wherever the voices and rhythm make tracking easy. If voices are well differentiated, three speakers can run untagged for a surprising stretch. Re-tag after a beat of action, or when more than a few lines have passed.

Elmore Leonard's tenth rule — *never use a verb other than "said"* — is overstated as law and useful as default. Break it when breaking it earns the attention.

## On-the-nose detection

Dialogue is on-the-nose when characters say what they feel, explain what just happened, or tell each other things they both already know. The diagnostic markers:

- **Emotional labelling.** *"I'm so angry at you for what you did."* Real angry people rarely announce the anger; they enact it. Cut the label; render the behaviour.
- **Plot recap.** *"As you know, our father died last spring and left us this house."* *As you know* (and its disguised cousins) is the marker. Characters do not exposit at each other for the reader's benefit.
- **Motive announcement.** *"I'm only saying this because I love you."* If the love is on the page, the line is redundant; if it is not, the line will not establish it.

The fix is almost always the same: cut the on-the-nose line and trust the surrounding scene to carry the meaning. If it does not, the problem is the scene, not the dialogue.

## Dialect and period handling

The modern light hand: a touch of vocabulary, a touch of syntax, a few rhythmic markers — and trust the reader. Heavy phonetic spelling (*"Oi was goin' down t'pub, weren't oi?"*) reads as caricature now. Toni Morrison handles African American Vernacular English in *Beloved* with restraint and exactitude — the rhythm and idiom are unmistakable; phonetic respelling is almost absent. Cormac McCarthy renders nineteenth-century border speech in *Blood Meridian* through cadence and vocabulary, not through *thar* and *reckon*.

Period dialogue has the same trap. *Sounding period* — Ye Olde Forsooth — is parody. *Being period* is quieter: avoiding anachronism in vocabulary and concept (no character in 1840 has *issues* with their mother), letting a few period-specific terms carry weight, otherwise writing prose your reader can read. Hilary Mantel's *Wolf Hall* feels Tudor without performing Tudor.

## Silence and beat as dialogue

What a character refuses to say is part of the conversation. The pause before answering, the change of subject, the question met with a question — these are lines, even with no words on the page.

*[Example crafted for illustration]* **"Did you tell him?" / She rinsed the cup. / "It's late."** Three exchanges, one silent. The rinse is an answer; the deflection is another; together they are the scene. Marilynne Robinson's *Gilead* uses this constantly — the unspoken half of every conversation between father and son.

A beat works as a line when it carries meaning the speaker chooses not to put into words. A beat is filler when it does not. The diagnostic: cut it. If the exchange loses something, it was a line. If nothing is lost, it was stage business.

## Dialogue-only scenes

A scene composed almost entirely of dialogue, with minimal beats and no interiority, is high-risk. It works when voices are sharply differentiated, subtext is dense, and stakes are clear from elsewhere — Higgins's novels, much of Rooney, the great interrogation chapters of detective fiction. It fails when any of those conditions are absent: voices blur, subtext thins, the reader cannot place the scene because no one is in a body in a room.

The trap is that dialogue-only scenes feel fast to write. The voices carry the writer along, and the writer mistakes the carrying for craft. Read the scene aloud. If you cannot tell the speakers apart with your eyes shut, the scene needs work — beats, interiority, or differentiation — before it earns the technique.
