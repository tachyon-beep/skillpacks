# Pacing and Tension

Pacing is how fast the reader moves through the prose; tension is the reason they want to keep moving. The two are entangled but distinct, and most diagnoses of "pacing problems" turn out to be tension problems wearing pacing's clothes. A short scene can drag; a long scene can pull. The question is rarely *how many words* — it is *what is the reader hoping for, dreading, or unsure of*, and *is that pressure building*.

Tension is not the same as action. Quiet novels have tension; action-packed novels can be tensionless. Chases and ticking clocks do not produce pull; *something the reader wants and does not yet have* produces pull.

## Microtension vs macrotension

Two scales operate at once.

**Macrotension** is arc-level drive — the question that opens early and resolves late. *Who killed Roger Ackroyd?* *Will Lily Bart survive her social descent?* Macrotension is what the writer pitches when they describe the book.

**Microtension** is paragraph-level pull — the small frictions, withholdings, and unease that keep the reader's eye moving from sentence to sentence. Donald Maass's framing of microtension is one named lens for it (workshop-cited, not commandment): in any given paragraph, *something* should be unsettled — a discrepancy between what the character says and feels, a gesture that does not match the words, a sentence that almost-but-not-quite lands where it seemed to be heading.

Either alone is insufficient. A novel with strong macrotension and dead microtension is the book the reader claims they "couldn't get into" despite being interested in the premise. A novel with sparkling microtension and no macrotension is the book they admire one paragraph at a time and then close after thirty pages because nothing is *pulling* them through.

## Three withholding modes

Tension is, mostly, a withholding mechanic. Three modes, each working differently:

**Withholding** — the reader does not have the information at all. The opening of Donna Tartt's *The Secret History* tells us a death has happened; it does not tell us why. The reader reads forward to find out.

**Delaying** — the reader has been promised the information and is waiting for it. *[example crafted for illustration: a chapter ends "She would tell him, but not tonight." The reader now reads forward toward a scene that has been named.]* The promise is the engine.

**Concealing** — the reader thinks they have the information but they do not. Gillian Flynn's *Gone Girl* runs on this; so does most of Patricia Highsmith's work. The reader is not waiting for a revelation; they are constructing a misreading the writer will later collapse. Concealing is the most ethically charged of the three because it depends on the reader trusting a frame the writer is preparing to break.

A draft in which the writer mostly *withholds* feels mysterious; a draft that mostly *delays* feels propulsive; a draft that mostly *conceals* feels (when it works) like vertigo. Most strong novels braid all three.

## Suspense vs mystery

Hitchcock's distinction is durable. Two characters sit at a table; under it, a bomb is ticking. If the audience does not know the bomb is there and it goes off — that is *surprise*, ten seconds of shock. If the audience knows the bomb is there and the characters do not — that is *suspense*, ten minutes of unbearable conversation.

Mystery (the reader does not know) and suspense (the reader knows; the characters do not) generate different kinds of pull and fit different projects. A whodunnit lives on mystery. A thriller often lives on suspense. Le Carré's *Tinker Tailor Soldier Spy* is mostly mystery (who is the mole?); Highsmith's *Strangers on a Train* is mostly suspense (we know what Bruno will do; Guy does not). Choosing which mode dominates a given chapter is a real craft decision, not a default.

## Sentence rhythm as pace

Pace at the sentence level is mostly rhythm. Short sentences accelerate. Long sustained sentences slow. The effect operates at the nervous-system level — the reader's breath, conscious or not, tracks the punctuation. A page of clipped action sentences reads as fast even when the events depicted are not. A page of clause-stacked Henry James sentences reads as slow even in moments of internal crisis.

The craft is fitting rhythm to scene. Action and panic want short, declarative, often fragmentary syntax. Reflection and dread want sustained sentences whose grammar refuses to let the reader exit. Cormac McCarthy's chase scenes punch; Marilynne Robinson's reflections in *Gilead* unspool. Both are pacing decisions made in syntax. See `prose-rhythm-and-style` for the sentence-level craft.

## Chapter-end mechanics

Chapter endings teach the reader what the book is doing. Three common modes:

**The unresolved** — the chapter closes on a question, a held breath, an unanswered note. *[example crafted for illustration: "She heard the gate open. She did not turn around."]* The reader turns the page because the moment is suspended.

**The cliffhanger** — a sharp interruption at maximum jeopardy. Genre fiction earns its reputation here; Lee Child's Reacher novels close chapters this way as a matter of course, and the technique is honest about what it is.

**The lyrical close** — the chapter ends on an image or cadence that does not propel but resonates. Marilynne Robinson and Kazuo Ishiguro end chapters this way; the reader pauses, then continues because the resonance has set up the next chapter's silence.

None of these is correct in the abstract. A novel that uses only cliffhangers exhausts; a novel that uses only lyrical closes loses propulsion. The chapter-end is a tool; the writer chooses.

## Why middles drag

The middle-of-novel slump is almost always a tension problem, not a length problem. Cutting words rarely fixes it. Diagnostic questions, in order:

- Does the reader know what is going to happen next? If yes, a withholding has collapsed; restore one.
- Has anything *new* been withheld in the last fifty pages? If no, the reader is coasting on questions raised long ago.
- Have the stakes escalated, or have they only repeated? Repetition reads as treading water.
- Is the character making decisions, or being moved by events? Passive middles drag worst.

The fix is usually *introduce a new question, raise the stakes of an old one, or force a decision*, not *cut twenty pages*.

## The tension-not-action distinction

Quiet domestic novels have enormous tension. Marilynne Robinson's *Gilead* is a dying man writing a letter to his young son; almost nothing external happens; the tension is unbearable. Henry James's *The Portrait of a Lady* runs on tension that is almost entirely interior — what Isabel Archer will choose, what she has begun to suspect, what she refuses to acknowledge. Tana French's *In the Woods* has external action but its tension is the narrator's relationship to a memory he cannot recover.

Tension comes from withholding, from stakes, from unresolved want. It does not come from chases. A draft whose middle drags should not, as a first move, add a chase. It should ask: *what does the protagonist want that she does not have, and what is she beginning to fear?* Make those sharper. Action follows; sometimes action is unnecessary altogether.
