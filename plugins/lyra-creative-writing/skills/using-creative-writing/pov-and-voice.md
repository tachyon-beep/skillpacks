# POV and Voice

Point-of-view is not a rule, it is a contract. The narrator promises the reader what they will and will not know, whose head they will live in, how close they will sit to the action, and what kind of attention the prose is asking for. Every other craft choice in the scene either honours that contract or breaks it. Slips matter because the contract matters; the contract matters because that is what the reader gave themselves over to in the first paragraph.

## The choices

**First person** — *I* tells the story. The narrator is a character with their own voice, blind spots, and unreliability. The risk is sustained-voice fatigue and the limit of one consciousness; the gift is intimacy and persona. Shirley Jackson's *We Have Always Lived in the Castle* and Marlon James's *A Brief History of Seven Killings* show first person at very different settings: the former a single fragile narrator, the latter a chorus of them.

**Second person** — *You* puts the reader inside the action. Rare because it strains to sustain; powerful when the strain is itself the subject. N.K. Jemisin's *The Fifth Season* moves into and out of second person deliberately; the choice is structural, not a flourish.

**Third limited (close)** — *She* told from inside one character's head, with access to her thoughts, sensations, and feelings. The reader knows what the POV character knows, when she knows it. Tana French's *In the Woods* is close third; so is most contemporary commercial and literary fiction. Default for a reason: it gives the writer most of first person's intimacy with most of third's flexibility.

**Third limited (distant)** — *She* told from outside, observing. The narrator may know things the character does not, but reports them at a remove. Voice belongs to the prose more than to the character. Edith Wharton frequently sits at this distance; so does much classic short fiction.

**Third omniscient** — *They* told by a narrator who knows everything about everyone, and whose voice is its own performance. George Eliot's *Middlemarch* is omniscient; so is Tolstoy. Out of fashion for most of the late twentieth century, due a comeback when the writer's voice can carry the load. Hard to do well; unmistakable when it is.

## Distance

Within third person there is a continuum from *very close* (the prose almost in the character's voice, thought rendered as if spoken inside her head) to *very distant* (the narrator reporting from a great height). Distance is not fixed for a whole novel; it can move within a chapter, within a scene, even within a paragraph — but only deliberately. *Whiplash* between distances inside a single passage reads as drift, not technique.

Distance also gates which interiority is available. Close third can render thought directly. Distant third must report it. Very distant third can only infer it from behaviour and dialogue. A scene that wants the texture of a character's mind must be close enough for the mind to be on the page; a scene that wants restraint should pull back so the mind is not on the page.

## Free indirect discourse

Free indirect discourse is the technique by which third-person narration takes on the voice of the POV character without quotation marks or attributive tags. Instead of *She thought, "I'll never finish this"*, it is: *She would never finish this.* The word-choice and rhythm belong to the character, but the grammar belongs to the narrator. It is the engine of close third — what makes voice possible from outside one consciousness — and it is half of what readers mean when they say "the prose has voice."

The marker is register-shift: a character's vocabulary, idiom, or syntax intruding into otherwise neutral narration. Jane Austen perfected it; Saunders teaches it. Every close-third writer needs it.

## Slip patterns

POV breaks have names. Naming them helps in revision.

- **Head-hopping.** Mid-paragraph or mid-scene jump from one character's interior to another's. *She thought he was lying. He thought she was beautiful.* The contract said one head; we got two.
- **Knowledge violations.** The POV character "knows" something she could not know — what is in another room, what another character is feeling, what is about to happen. *She didn't realise he was watching her with concern* is a common formulation: it imports the watcher's interior into a sentence that is supposedly hers.
- **Distance whiplash.** A close-third paragraph that suddenly zooms out to a paragraph of distant narrator-overview, then zooms back. Sometimes this is technique (cinematic pull-back); usually it is drift.
- **Filter words.** *She felt, she saw, she noticed, she realised* place a layer between the reader and the experience. They distance prose that is otherwise close, and they read as POV slack — the reader is being told what the character experienced rather than experiencing it with her.
- **Intrusive narrator in close third.** A piece of authorial commentary that the close-third character would not make. The narrator briefly steps out from inside her head to be the writer; readers feel the bump.

## Voice — character versus stylist

Two legitimate sources of voice:

**Voice from the character.** First person and close third draw their voice from the POV character's diction, syntax, what she notices, and what she refuses to say. The narrator is recognisable because the *character* is recognisable. Most contemporary fiction lives here.

**Voice from the stylist.** Distant third and omniscient draw voice from the prose itself — from sentence rhythm, vocabulary, attitude toward the material. The narrator is recognisable because the *writer* is. Wharton's voice is Wharton's, regardless of which character is in view; Tolstoy's is Tolstoy's.

Neither is better. They are different jobs. A novel can use both, but typically commits to one as the spine.

## Workshop framing — heuristic, not law

POV "rules" are a contract, not a commandment. Writers break them deliberately and beautifully all the time: omniscient passages in otherwise close-third novels (Marilynne Robinson does this), present-tense sections inside past-tense books, deliberate head-hops as character moments. The discipline is not *never break POV*; it is *know what you are promising and know when you are renegotiating*. A break the reader notices as drift is broken; a break the reader notices as technique is craft.
