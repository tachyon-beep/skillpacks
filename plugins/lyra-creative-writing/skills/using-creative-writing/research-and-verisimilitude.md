# Research and Verisimilitude

The point of researching for fiction is not to be right. It is to be *convincing* — and the difference is craft, not pedantry. A novel can be wrong in small ways the reader never notices and right in one detail that makes the world feel solid underfoot. Research is the work of finding that detail and earning the right to use it.

## Researching for fiction is not journalism

Journalism collects to report. The reporter's notebook fills with quotes and sources; the published piece tries to put as much of that on the page as the form will hold. Verification is the spine. The reader is owed the facts.

Fiction collects to *forget*. The novelist reads the regimental history, walks the ground, interviews the retired surgeon — and then writes a scene that uses almost none of it. What ends up on the page is one image, one cadence, one piece of jargon used unselfconsciously. The rest is the soil the scene grew in. The reader is not owed the research; the reader is owed the *feeling that the writer knows*.

This is why "I researched it for six months" is not, by itself, a craft achievement. The achievement is the selection.

## Knowing more than you put on the page

The iceberg principle, as Hemingway named it for prose generally, applies sharply to research. Ten percent on the page; ninety percent in the writer's head. The reader feels the ninety percent through the ten — through the confidence of the prose, through what is *not* explained, through the offhand reference offered without elaboration because the narrator (and behind her the writer) does not feel the need to elaborate.

The test of the iceberg is asymmetry. Know one fact and put it on the page: the prose feels brittle and tour-guide. Know fifty and put one on the page: that same fact feels load-bearing. Same sentence; different gravity.

## Period-detail discipline

The discipline is selection. Hilary Mantel's *Wolf Hall* is the canonical case for restraint: the Tudor world is dense and immediate, but the density is built from the *one* image — the wet wool of a cloak, a dog under a table, a clerk's chilblained hands — rather than the ten. Mantel's research was prodigious; her deployment of it is small.

Annie Proulx works the same discipline on landscape. *The Shipping News* and the Wyoming stories are saturated with vocational and ecological specificity, but each scene picks one or two terms (a particular knot, a particular weather word) and lets them stand for the whole. The reader does not get the marine encyclopedia. The reader gets the cleat-hitch and trusts the rest.

Near-future fiction has the same discipline. *[Example crafted for illustration]* — a 2040 scene anchored by a single offhand reference to the new building code, mentioned without explanation because the character has lived under it for a decade, will land harder than a paragraph that walks the reader through how the building code came about. One detail, deeply known. The rest, off-page.

## Anachronism — kinds and severity

Three kinds, in roughly increasing severity and decreasing visibility.

**Material anachronism.** A wristwatch in 1815. A zipper before 1893. A character lighting a friction match before 1826. Catchable by a careful reader and by any specialist; embarrassing when caught; usually cheap to fix. These are the ones writers stress about most and that matter least.

**Linguistic anachronism.** A 21st-century idiom in a Victorian's mouth — *I'm good*, *no worries*, *reach out*. Catchable by an attentive reader; ruinous to the spell of the prose; harder to fix because the writer's own ear is contemporary and the modern phrasing slips in unremarked. Worth a dedicated revision pass.

**Conceptual anachronism.** A 19th-century character thinking about her marriage in the vocabulary and emotional grammar of 21st-century therapy culture. A medieval peasant evaluating her station with modern political consciousness. This is the easiest to miss — the writer cannot see past her own conceptual furniture — and the hardest to fix, because fixing it means rebuilding the character's interior from materials the writer does not naturally reach for. Mantel's Cromwell does not think in twenty-first-century terms; that restraint is the whole novel.

## Technical material without infodumps

Vocational fiction lives or dies on this. The reader needs to feel that the writer has been there, but if the writer *demonstrates* having been there, the spell breaks. The marker of expertise on the page is jargon used *unselfconsciously* — the way a working cardiologist uses *PCI* or a working litigator uses *motion in limine*, without translation, without italics, because the speaker assumes the listener knows.

Patrick O'Brian's Aubrey-Maturin novels are the high-water mark for naval fiction. The ships are crewed in nineteenth-century vocabulary, much of it unglossed, and the reader either picks it up or does not — and the prose's confidence makes most readers pick it up. Tana French does this with police procedural cadence — the way detectives talk to each other, the rhythm of a room, what is *not* said. Atul Gawande, in creative nonfiction, renders surgery the same way: he is a surgeon writing for surgeons who are happy to be read by laypeople, not a journalist explaining surgery to outsiders. The register matters.

The other half of expertise on the page is what the expert does *not* say. The surgeon does not pause to describe the OR. The detective does not narrate the procedure. The omission is the proof.

## When to invent and when to verify

Heuristics, not rules.

- **Fictional towns: yes.** Faulkner's Yoknapatawpha, Hardy's Wessex. Inventing geography frees the writer from logistics and from local readers' corrections.
- **Dates of historical events: no.** If Waterloo is in your novel it is on 18 June 1815. The reader who notices the wrong date is the reader who cared.
- **Precise specifications of real weapons, aircraft, drugs, surgical procedures: no.** Genre readers will catch it and will not forgive. The Glock holds what it holds; the dosage is the dosage.
- **Emotional, sensory, interior reactions to those things: yes.** Nobody can correct your character's experience of being shot at. That is what fiction is for.

The shape of the rule: verify what is *checkable* and external; invent what is *unfalsifiable* and internal. The line between is where the craft happens.

## Research-as-procrastination

Research can become a way of not writing. The library is more comforting than the blank page, and the next book on the period is always available. The diagnostic question, asked honestly: *am I researching to find out, or researching to delay?*

If you cannot remember a specific question you are trying to answer, you are delaying. If your "research" has not changed anything in the draft for two weeks, you are delaying. If you find yourself reading around the topic rather than at it, you are delaying.

Stop when you have enough to draft the next scene. Draft. Mark gaps with brackets — *[check the dosage]*, *[name of the regiment]* — and keep going. The gaps will be there when revision comes. The draft will not write itself in your absence.
