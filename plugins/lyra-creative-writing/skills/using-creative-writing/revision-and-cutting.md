# Revision and Cutting

Revision is where books are made. First drafts are generation; revision is the act of seeing what has been generated and deciding what it is. The writers who talk most about craft — King, Le Guin, Atwood, Gardner — talk most about revision because that is where craft visibly lives. The first draft is private. The revision is the work.

The discipline of revision is the discipline of *order*: doing the right pass at the right time, on the right scale, and not the wrong one.

## Pass-based revision

Structural before line. Always. The reason is simple and the violation is universal: if you line-edit a paragraph that you later cut, you have wasted the line-edit *and* you have built attachment to the paragraph. Attachment is the enemy of cutting. A polished sentence is harder to delete than a rough one, even when both are equally extraneous. The order matters not just for efficiency but for psychology.

Most writers know this. Most violate it anyway, because line-editing feels like progress and structural revision feels like staring into a void. The discipline is to do the harder pass first.

## The four-pass model

Not a rigid sequence; a useful schema. King's *On Writing* describes two formal drafts (open-door, closed-door) plus polish; Le Guin's *Steering the Craft* assumes the structural work is mostly done before the line work begins. The four-pass version is the workshop articulation.

**Pass 1 — Structural.** Does the book work? Are the scenes pulling weight? Does the arc land? Are characters consistent across the manuscript? Are there scenes that should not exist, and missing scenes that should? This pass cuts and adds whole scenes; it does not fix sentences. *In scope:* scene order, scene presence/absence, arc, pacing at the manuscript level, plot logic, character consistency. *Out of scope:* prose, dialogue polish, word choice.

**Pass 2 — Scene.** Does each remaining scene justify itself? (See `scene-construction`.) Is each entered late and exited early? Is the POV right? Is the right material on-page and the right material off-page? *In scope:* scene-level cuts, scene-level POV decisions, beat order within scenes. *Out of scope:* sentence rhythm.

**Pass 3 — Line.** Sentence-level prose. Filter words, weak verbs, throat-clearing, redundancy, cliché, paragraph rhythm, dialogue attribution. The pass that line-reviewers are for. *In scope:* every sentence. *Out of scope:* whether the scene should exist (decided already).

**Pass 4 — Polish.** Typos, punctuation, consistency of names and ages and dates, formatting. The final read-through, ideally on paper, ideally aloud. *In scope:* errors. *Out of scope:* anything you would call revision.

The four passes are not four read-throughs; serious manuscripts get more. But the *order of concerns* is the spine.

## Kill your darlings without sentimentality

The line is attributed to Faulkner, Wilde, and Stephen King; it actually originates with Sir Arthur Quiller-Couch in 1914: *"Murder your darlings."* The principle is older than any of them.

The darling is the sentence, paragraph, scene, or character you love disproportionately to its contribution. It may be the most beautiful prose in the manuscript. The test is not *is it good?* — it usually is. The test is: *does it pay its way in this draft, regardless of how beautiful it is in isolation?* If a passage's beauty depends on context the manuscript no longer provides, or makes a point the manuscript no longer needs, it is a darling, and beauty alone is not enough.

The advanced version: most darlings should be moved to a "deleted scenes" file rather than truly deleted. Some return — to this manuscript, to a later draft, to a different book entirely. The cut is from this draft, not from your work. That distinction makes the cut survivable.

## What to cut

A non-exhaustive list:

- **Filter words:** *she felt, she saw, she heard, she noticed, she realised.* (See `pov-and-voice`.)
- **Throat-clearing openings:** *It was a, There were, She found herself.* The sentence usually starts one beat later.
- **Redundant beats:** the second time the character is angry on the same page; the third time you describe the rain.
- **Processing-loop interiority:** the character thinking through what just happened, when the reader has already understood it. (See `character-interiority`.)
- **Exposition the reader has already inferred** — usually two paragraphs after the inference was available.
- **Backstory that is not load-bearing.** The fact a reader needs is the one that pays off later. Everything else is the writer reassuring themselves.
- **Adjective stacks:** *the tall, dark, brooding, silent man.* Pick one. Better, replace with a verb.
- **Double-attribution:** *"I hate you," she said angrily.* The dialogue carries the anger; the adverb undermines it.
- **Dialogue tags doing emotional work the dialogue should do:** *she snarled, he hissed, she cried.* Mostly: *said*. Le Guin is firm on this.

## The over-polished early-draft trap

The most common form: revising the same paragraph twenty times and not moving forward. It feels like work; it produces nothing. The paragraph may not survive the structural pass.

The tell is small revisions to early chapters while later chapters remain unwritten or untouched. The fix is the four-pass discipline: in a structural pass, you are not allowed to fix sentences. You are looking for what stays and what goes. Sentences are someone else's job — your later self's.

This applies to feedback too. A line-edit on a scene that should be cut is wasted on both sides.

## Reading the manuscript as a stranger

The screen lies. Eyes track familiar passages without seeing them; the brain auto-corrects. To revise well you have to defamiliarise the text.

- **Print it.** Paper reads differently from screen. Errors become visible.
- **Change the font.** A draft set in Garamond reads differently from the same draft in Courier. The brain treats it as new material.
- **Read aloud.** This is the single highest-leverage revision technique. Sentences that do not work do not sound right. (See `prose-rhythm-and-style`.) Le Guin's whole pedagogy turns on this; King reads aloud at the editing stage; most working writers do.
- **Set it aside.** Six weeks, minimum, between final draft and serious revision. King recommends *at least* six weeks in the drawer. Distance is what makes you a stranger to your own prose.

Each technique works because the brain stops auto-completing.

## When *not* to revise

There is a point past which revision stops improving the manuscript and starts replacing it. Symptoms: the prose gets quieter and more competent and less alive; the rough vivid sentence is sanded into the smooth dead sentence; the writer cannot remember what the book was originally for.

There is also revision-as-avoidance: revising forever to avoid finishing, or to avoid showing the work to anyone. The literary writer's twenty-year novel is sometimes a masterpiece and more often a manuscript that stopped being readable in year nine. Atwood, asked about this: at some point you let the book go because the alternative is never letting it go, and the latter is worse.

The signal is simple. If a revision pass adds nothing — if the changes are sideways rather than forward — the book is done. Send it.
