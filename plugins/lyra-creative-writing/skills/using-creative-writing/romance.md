# Romance

## The contract

Romance readers come expecting the central romantic relationship to resolve with the couple together at the end — Happily Ever After (HEA) or Happy For Now (HFN). The contract is held more strictly than any other genre's. RWA and the trade bodies that descend from it write HEA/HFN into the definition of *romance*; bookstore aisles enforce it; agents and editors enforce it; readers police it harder than mystery readers police fair-play. The contract is about the *central* relationship, not every relationship in the book — secondary couples can fall apart, parents can die, friendships can rupture — but the central love story has to land where readers were promised it would land.

The open↔closed-door axis is a per-book choice with strong subgenre conventions: contemporary, romantasy, and dark romance run more open than they did twenty years ago; inspirational, regency, and cosy contemporary remain largely closed. Open or closed, the HEA/HFN contract is the same.

## Subgenres and their secondary contracts

Each romance subgenre has its own conventions stacked atop HEA/HFN. Regency (Heyer's invention, contemporary heirs from Mary Balogh to Tessa Dare) carries period-detail discipline and strong constraints on what is sayable. Contemporary inherits the most flexibility on register and content. Paranormal, romantic suspense, sports, dark, and romantasy each carry their own reader expectations — readers of a Karin Slaughter–style romantic suspense expect HEA *and* a credible body count; romantasy readers expect HEA *and* fantasy worldbuilding that honours its own rules; dark romance readers tolerate content (kidnapping, captivity tropes, dub-con) that contemporary readers will not.

Knowing your subgenre is craft. Pretending genre-fluidity will let you skip the conventions readers picked the book up for is not.

## The cost of breaking HEA/HFN

Severe and direct. Romance is the most marketed-to-readers-by-contract genre in the industry. A book without HEA/HFN cannot be sold as romance — RWA-descended trade bodies define it out, the romance imprints reject it, the romance reviewers down-vote it, the bookstore shelving algorithm sends it to women's fiction or upmarket literary or general fiction. The author's existing romance readership rarely follows; a romance writer who breaks the contract once usually has to rebuild a different audience under a different lane (and sometimes a different name). Prize ecology: the RITAs (and the new Vivian after RWA's 2020 reorganisation) explicitly require HEA/HFN. A book without the contract is not eligible.

The cost is the reshelve and the lost readership. The book exists; it just exists in a different aisle, with different agents, different editors, different comp titles, different review outlets. Some writers fight to keep tragic-love-story books in the romance category and lose. Most accept the reshelve.

## Books worth the cost

*The Bridges of Madison County* (Robert James Waller, 1992) — central romance ends in permanent separation. Waller never tried to sell it as romance. It became one of the most commercially successful love stories of the late twentieth century by accepting the reshelve as literary/commercial.

*The Time Traveler's Wife* (Audrey Niffenegger, 2003) — romance with permanent loss; reshelved as upmarket literary. Word-of-mouth crossover that romance readers and literary readers both embraced, on different terms.

*One Day* (David Nicholls, 2009) — broke HEA in the final act; sold as upmarket commercial; the break is what the book is about.

*Me Before You* (Jojo Moyes, 2012) — terminal-illness love story that ends in loss. Moyes is *adjacent* to romance and writes for an overlapping readership; the book sat in upmarket commercial / book-club fiction. The cost was paid willingly: the book wanted to be about choice and dignity at the end of life, and HEA would have undercut the argument.

*Gone Girl* (Flynn, 2012) — central relationship resolves, but in horror and mutual capture rather than HEA. Reshelved as thriller; succeeded extraordinarily.

*Atonement* (McEwan, 2001) — the ending retroactively breaks the HEA the novel was teaching the reader to expect. Literary fiction, by design.

In each case the writer was not really writing a romance. They were using the romantic-relationship frame to do other work — argument, elegy, social critique, a study of regret. The reshelve was honest; the form they were actually working in was the form the book got marketed as.

## Crossing with fantasy (romantasy)

The dominant cross-genre romance form in the current market (Sarah J. Maas, Rebecca Yarros, Carissa Broadbent, Holly Black at the literary end). Both contracts honoured strictly: fantasy worldbuilding *and* HEA/HFN. Word counts run high (often 150k+) because dual contract demands satisfaction at both layers — the world has to work, the relationship has to land. The market shorthand "romance with fantasy" and "fantasy with romance" reflects which contract dominates: romantasy where romance dominates puts HEA non-negotiable; fantasy with romance lets the romance fail or fade if the fantasy story requires it.

## Crossing with thriller (romantic suspense)

HEA preserved; the suspense engine drives the plot. Mary Stewart's mid-century classics; Nora Roberts (especially the J.D. Robb In Death series); Karin Slaughter at the darker end where the genre approaches thriller proper. The thriller's stakes-escalation does not break HEA; the central couple still arrives. The reader buys both contracts and expects both delivered.

## Crossing with literary-fiction

Rare and tense. The literary tradition often resists the HEA contract on principle — A.S. Byatt's *Possession* threads literary credentials and a satisfying romance plot together but is never sold as romance; Rachel Cusk's *Outline* trilogy actively resists the romance plot in literary frame; Sally Rooney's *Normal People* honours something like an HFN at the cost of literary ambiguity readers debated for years. When literary fiction and romance cross successfully, the friction is often the point — the books are *about* whether the romance contract can hold under the conditions the literary frame insists on examining.
