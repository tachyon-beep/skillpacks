# Scene Construction

A scene is a unit of dramatised time. The reader is in the room with the characters; the prose renders moment-by-moment what is happening, what is being said, what is being felt. Anything else — backstory, summary of weeks or years, abstract reflection on the meaning of the action — is something else, and it serves the scene by *not* being the scene.

The single most useful diagnostic question for any draft is: *why does this scene exist?* If you cannot answer that in one sentence, the scene is in trouble. Not because it has to do "goal, conflict, disaster" (it does not, despite what every writing book says), but because every minute of dramatised time is expensive and the reader is paying.

## Scene versus summary versus sequel

**Scene** is dramatised time. *She picked up the knife. It was heavier than she remembered.* The reader is there.

**Summary** is condensed time. *The next three weeks passed in a blur of phone calls.* The reader is told. Summary is not failure; it is the right register for compression. A novel without summary is exhausting; a novel without scene is inert.

**Sequel** is the term Dwight Swain coined for the after-scene processing — the character thinking through what just happened, feeling its weight, deciding what to do next. Sometimes it is its own scene; sometimes it is a paragraph between scenes. The Swain *MRU* (motivation-reaction unit) framework prescribes alternation; in practice the alternation is looser, and "sequel" is a useful name for the texture rather than a beat to hit.

The diagnostic for any passage: *which is this?* If you have written a sentence-long summary in the middle of a scene without intending to, you have probably broken the dramatised continuity; if you have written a scene where summary would have been faster, you have probably stretched a moment that did not earn the stretch.

## The "every scene needs goal, conflict, disaster" formula

This is the workshop saw of the early 2000s, popularised by Jack M. Bickham and tightened by Donald Maass. The claim: every scene needs a POV character with a goal, an obstacle to that goal, and a setback at the end. The claim is *useful* for plot-driven commercial fiction and *misleading* for everything else.

Quiet domestic novels have scenes without external goals. Literary fiction often has scenes whose work is interior — a character realising something, a relationship shifting by one degree. Atmospheric fiction (much horror, much dream-logic literary work) has scenes whose job is to deepen mood, not to drive plot. A scene of two old friends having coffee in a Cairo café where nothing "happens" externally can be the load-bearing scene of a novel.

Use the formula as one diagnostic among several, not as the universal scene-skeleton. If a scene has goal/conflict/disaster, you should be able to name them — and if you cannot, that may be fine, depending on what kind of novel you are writing.

## Why this scene exists

The actually universal claim is broader: *every scene has to justify itself.* Multiple legitimate justifications:

- **Dramatising change** — something is different by the end of the scene than by the start. A relationship, a situation, a piece of information.
- **Deepening character** — the reader knows something about a character at the end of the scene that they did not know at the start. Not necessarily backstory; texture.
- **Complicating premise** — what looked one way looks another way now. The world is more difficult or more strange than it was a page ago.
- **Withholding information productively** — what is *not* said in the scene, what is *not* shown, builds pressure toward later revelation.
- **Establishing world** — the texture of a place, an era, a subculture. Done by implication; rarely by tour.
- **Building rhythm** — quiet scenes after loud ones; loud after quiet. Pacing at scene-level.

Most strong scenes do two or three of these at once. Scenes that do only one are usually fine. Scenes that do none — that exist because the writer needed to *get* the characters somewhere, or remembered a fact the reader needs to know — are the cuts.

## Entry late, exit early

The standard workshop counsel, attributed variously to William Goldman and to the screenwriting tradition: enter the scene as late as possible (just before the moment that matters) and exit as early as possible (just after the moment lands). Scenes typically begin too soon and end too late in early drafts.

A scene that opens with two characters arriving at a restaurant, ordering, making small talk, and *then* having the conversation that the scene is for has spent its first 400 words on logistics. Cut to the conversation. The reader will infer the arrival.

A scene that, having delivered its load-bearing exchange, then walks the characters back to the car, drives them home, and watches them arrive — has spent its last 300 words on logistics. Cut on the moment that mattered. Let the reader feel the silence after.

## On-the-page versus off-the-page action

Choosing what to dramatise and what to imply is the most consequential craft decision in any scene. The fight that happens off-page, reported in the next scene, is sometimes far more powerful than the fight rendered blow-by-blow. The death that happens between chapters can devastate; the death dramatised in detail can cheapen. (Or vice versa, depending on what the book is doing.)

The question is not *what do I want to render?* but *what does the reader need to feel here, and which choice — on-page or off-page — produces that feeling?* The answer is often counter-intuitive: the most charged moment often belongs off-page; the unbearable wait for it belongs on-page.

## Scene-level cause and effect

Scenes do not stand alone; they stand in sequence. Each scene exists *because of* a prior scene and *toward* a subsequent one. The connection need not be melodramatic ("X happened, so Y had to happen"); it is often a subtler chain of consequence — emotional, situational, informational.

The diagnostic: can you draw a line from this scene back to the previous one and forward to the next? If two scenes are interchangeable in order, one of them is probably not pulling its weight, or you have a structural seam.
