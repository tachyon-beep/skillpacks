# Science Fiction

## The contract

Science fiction readers come expecting a *novum* — Darko Suvin's term, taken up by the field — one rigorous change from current reality whose consequences follow logically and generate the story. The reader buys two things at once: a change worth thinking about, and a writer who has thought through what follows. Worldbuilding in SF is load-bearing, not decorative. The rules are made to bite. When they don't bite, the reader feels the slack immediately, the same way a mystery reader feels an unfair clue.

The novum can be technological (faster-than-light, AGI, uplift, generation ship), physical (altered gravity, altered chemistry, altered cosmology), social (post-scarcity, single-gender society, surveillance state taken to its limit), or biological (clones, symbionts, post-human bodies). Hard SF tends to centre the technological or physical novum; soft SF tends to centre the social or biological. Either way, the contract is the same: pick the change, follow it through, do not flinch from the second-order effects.

The detective's competence in mystery has an analogue here — the writer's *thoughtfulness*. The reader is trusting that someone has done the work: that if the novel posits a generation ship, the writer has thought about the politics of generation seven; that if it posits AGI, the writer has thought about labour. SF's deepest pleasure is the moment a consequence the reader had not predicted falls cleanly out of premises they were given on page one.

## The hard ↔ soft axis (and the literary-SF position)

The genre's deepest internal disagreement runs along two axes that interact.

**Hard ↔ soft.** Hard SF (Asimov, Clarke, Greg Egan, Andy Weir, Ted Chiang in his harder mode, Kim Stanley Robinson at his most procedural) foregrounds the science — the orbital mechanics, the chemistry, the evolutionary biology — and asks the reader to feel the rigour as part of the pleasure. Soft SF (Le Guin, Octavia Butler, Becky Chambers, much of the New Wave of the 1960s and 1970s) foregrounds anthropology, sociology, and lived consequence — what the novum does to a marriage, a city, a generation. Both poles honour novum-with-consequences. They differ on which consequences they choose to render.

**Literary-SF and the Le Guin/Atwood debate.** Then there is the question of category itself, and here the dispute is real and unresolved. Le Guin insisted on *science fiction* as the right name for her work and refused the offered exit into "speculative fiction" — for her, the genre needed defending and inhabiting, not transcending. Atwood preferred *speculative fiction* for *The Handmaid's Tale* and the MaddAddam trilogy, partly to mark a distance from rocket-and-rayguns SF, partly for market and prize reasons. They were doing similar authorial moves: literary credentials, SF subject. Different communities read those moves differently. The workshop position is to name the dispute and not pick a side. Both wrote SF. The disagreement is about category prestige and reception, not about craft. Ishiguro's *Klara and the Sun* (Booker-winning author, AI subject) and *Never Let Me Go* before it sit in the same room as the Le Guin/Atwood debate — literary-SF whose SF apparatus is treated loosely on purpose, and which SF readers and literary readers receive on different terms.

## The cost of breaking the novum-with-consequences contract

Concrete and felt. If the novum is decorative — there for setting flavour but its consequences ignored — the SF reader feels cheated in a way the literary reader often does not. Worldbuilding rigour is an explicit value in the genre's prize ecology: the Hugos, the Nebulas, the Clarke, the BSFA all assess it as part of craft, and a novel whose rules don't bite is rarely shortlisted. Cosy or feel-good SF (Becky Chambers won Hugos despite some hard-SF readers' grumbling) does not break the contract — it gives a softer novum genuine weight in characters' lives, which is a different choice, not a slack one.

When the novum collapses, books reshelve. As fantasy, when the rules stop biting and the apparatus becomes spectacle. As literary fiction, when the SF furniture is metaphor only. As YA dystopia, where rule-rigour is famously variable and readers have learned to forgive it. The reshelve is the price. It is not a moral failing. It is a cost.

## Books worth the cost

*The Left Hand of Darkness* (Le Guin, 1969) — broke the hard-SF dominant mode by foregrounding gender anthropology over technology. The novum was Gethen's biology; the consequences Le Guin chose to follow were social, intimate, and political. The book rewrote what soft SF could do and the field has been catching up since.

*The Road* (McCarthy, 2006) — broke novum-with-consequences-that-follow-logically by leaving the catastrophe deliberately unspecified. SF readers have argued for twenty years about whether it counts as SF; the Pulitzer committee did not care. Reshelved as literary; the unspecified novum is the point.

*Klara and the Sun* (Ishiguro, 2021) and *Never Let Me Go* (2005) — broke hard-extrapolation expectation. The Artificial Friend's mechanics, the cloning programme's biology — neither is rigorously worked. Ishiguro is doing something the SF apparatus is in service to, not the reverse. Booker-credentialled author, literary-SF reception, mostly a calm reshelve.

*Children of Time* (Tchaikovsky, 2015) — broke "human protagonist required" with uplifted spiders as one of the two POV strands, honoured every other contract scrupulously, won the Clarke. The break worked because the novum-rigour was never compromised; the consequences for spider cognition were thought through.

*Story of Your Life* (Chiang, 1998), filmed as *Arrival* — broke linear narrative under the novum's logic. The Heptapod language reshapes time; the prose reshapes accordingly. The form follows the rules. The clean instance of an SF story whose structural choice is *itself* a consequence of the novum.

Each break was made for a craft reason. Each writer knew the price.

## Crossing with literary-fiction

The most established cross now, and the site of the Le Guin/Atwood debate. Le Guin's late work, Atwood's MaddAddam, Ishiguro's *Klara* and *Never Let Me Go*, Chiang's *Stories of Your Life and Others* and *Exhalation*, Emily St. John Mandel's *Station Eleven* and *Sea of Tranquility* — all sit in the literary-SF room. Contract collisions to know: SF wants the consequences worked; literary tolerates the apparatus held loose. Plot-clue density runs against interiority for word-budget. SF readers and literary readers want different things from the last forty pages — closure of consequence versus a registered ambiguity. Successful literary-SF tends either to honour rigour and let the literary commitments live in interiority (Chiang) or to declare the apparatus loose up front and let the metaphor carry (Ishiguro).

## Crossing with fantasy (the SFF umbrella)

Marketed together as SFF since the 1970s; argued about ever since. The boundary debate is real: soft SF and fantasy meet at the place where the rules stop being scientific and start being magical, and the line is genuinely contested. Vance's *Dying Earth* sits on the line by design. Wolfe's *Book of the New Sun* is far-future SF that reads as fantasy. Le Guin worked both rooms. The umbrella is a marketing convenience and an honest description of overlapping readerships.

## Crossing with horror

Cosmic horror is the SF/horror border. Lovecraft made the move first — vast indifferent cosmologies as the source of dread, the human scale insufficient to the rules. VanderMeer's *Annihilation* honours an SF novum (Area X) and lets the dread rather than the deduction dominate. Liu Cixin's *The Three-Body Problem* trilogy reaches the dark forest hypothesis as both rigorous SF extrapolation and cosmic-horror conclusion. The contract negotiation: when the novum is too large for human cognition to model, the puzzle contract bends toward the dread contract, and books that succeed let the dread lead.
