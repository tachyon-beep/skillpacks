# Showing vs Telling

"Show, don't tell" is the most quoted maxim in creative writing and one of the most misused. It started as a course-correction against the inert Victorian habit of telling the reader how to feel, and it hardened over a century into a commandment that, taken at face value, would forbid summary, voice, compression, and most of the great omniscient novels of the language. The discipline is not to obey the dictum but to know what it is actually pointing at, and where it stops being true.

## The actual distinction

Showing and telling are not opposites; they are two registers a narrator can use, and a working scene moves between them constantly. The useful distinction is **filtered narration vs scene rendering** and, at a longer scale, **summary vs dramatisation**.

*Filtered narration* puts a layer of report between the reader and the experience: *She was nervous about the meeting.* The narrator is paraphrasing the character's interior state for the reader. *Scene rendering* puts the reader inside the moment: *Her hand kept finding the edge of the folder. She had read the first page four times.* Same information; different register.

*Summary* compresses time: *The next year was the worst of her life.* *Dramatisation* slows time to a moment: *She put the kettle on, and then she sat down at the kitchen table and did not move.* Both are legitimate; they do different work. A novel made of pure dramatisation is exhausting and slow. A novel made of pure summary is inert. The craft is choosing the right register for the moment, not preferring one wholesale.

## When telling is the correct choice

Telling is the correct register for:

- **Compression of time.** *Three years passed.* You do not dramatise three years. Marilynne Robinson's *Gilead* tells huge stretches of a life in summary because the novel is a letter from a dying man; the telling *is* the form.
- **Voice-rich narration.** A first-person stylist or omniscient narrator earns the right to tell because the *voice doing the telling* is itself the pleasure. Stephen King's narrators tell constantly — *That was the year Eddie Kaspbrak's mother started worrying about everything* — and the voice carries it. Edith Wharton's omniscient narrator tells what her characters cannot articulate, and the prose is sharper for it.
- **Transitions.** The hinge between two dramatised scenes is almost always told. *They did not speak again for a week.*
- **Backstory whose details would distract.** If a fact is needed but the scene of its origin is not, tell it and move on. Le Guin tells huge swathes of backstory in *The Left Hand of Darkness* and the novel is faster for it, not slower.

The genre-and-literary pairing applies: Tana French's first-person detectives tell the reader things constantly because *the voice* is the case being made for the book; so does Marilynne Robinson's narrator in *Housekeeping*. Different registers, same technique.

## Filter words

*She felt the cold. He saw the door open. They noticed someone behind them. I realised I was lost.* Filter words name a perceiving consciousness in the act of perceiving, and in close third or first person they almost always weaken the prose. They put the character between the reader and the experience instead of letting the reader have it directly.

*She felt the floorboards cold under her feet.* → *The floorboards were cold under her feet.*

The second sentence is *closer*: the cold is in the reader's experience, not reported as her perception of it. In close third the POV is already established, so naming the perceiver again is redundant overhead. Filter words are not banned — sometimes the act of perceiving is itself the point (*She realised, only then, that he had been lying for months*) — but most of the time they should come out in revision.

## Abstract emotion-naming

*She was sad. He was angry. She felt afraid.* The flat name of an emotion is almost always weaker than the rendering of it. The alternatives, all available:

- **Behaviour.** What does she do that a sad person does, that this sad person in particular does?
- **Body.** Heat, cold, weight, breath, the place where grief sits in this character.
- **Perception.** A sad character notices different things in the room than a happy one. Filter the world through the feeling.
- **Dialogue.** What she says, what she refuses to say, what she says instead.
- **Free indirect discourse.** *It would be fine. Everything would be fine.* — and the reader knows nothing is fine.

*She was angry at him* → *She rinsed the cup again. The cup was clean.* The second sentence renders the anger by what it makes her do, and lets the reader name the feeling for themselves — which is most of why showing works when it works.

## Showing without melodrama

Showing does not mean cranking every emotion to maximum visibility. The over-rendered school — every feeling staged with trembling hands, racing hearts, and tight throats — is its own failure mode. Quiet showing is often stronger:

*She stood in the doorway with her hand on the frame.*

That is showing. Nothing is named; nothing is dramatised at volume; the gesture does the work. Or a sentence that breaks off:

*She was going to tell him. She was going to —*

The unfinished sentence is showing. Restraint is showing. Shirley Jackson's terror works almost entirely by quiet showing; so does Kazuo Ishiguro's grief. The hand on the doorframe is more felt than the racing heart, because the reader has to lean in.

## The maxim's failure modes

The dictum harms the work when it is applied wholesale. Watch for:

- **Over-rendered prose.** Every paragraph dramatised, no summary, no compression. Pacing dies.
- **Lost voice.** The narrator becomes a camera, recording action neutrally. First-person and omniscient narrators *should* tell; muting them in service of "showing" strips out the thing that made the prose alive.
- **Logistics rendered.** Characters arriving, ordering, making small talk, walking from room to room — all dramatised because the writer was told never to tell. Cut to the moment that matters.
- **Backstory infodump-as-flashback.** Dramatising a memory at full scene-length, when one summary sentence would have done the same work in a tenth of the space.
- **Emotional micromanagement.** Showing every feeling to the reader so insistently that the reader is never trusted to feel anything themselves. Telling, at times, gives the reader more room than showing does.

The honest version of the maxim: *render what needs to be felt; tell what needs to be known and moved past; trust the reader.* Showing is a default for moments of pressure, not a universal commandment.
